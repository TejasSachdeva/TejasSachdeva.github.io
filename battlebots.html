Battle Bots

Custom Built Remote-Controlled Racing Vehicle

Mechanical Design Electronics Control Systems 3D Printing

Project Overview

This project involved the design and fabrication of a combat robot tailored for durability and control in a collegiate BattleBots competition. Unlike conventional designs that prioritize aggressive weapon systems, our approach focused on building a reinforced chassis and a highly reliable drive framework. The goal was to maximize survivability against high-energy impacts, maintain consistent mobility throughout matches, and ensure serviceability for rapid adjustments between rounds. The resulting design served as a platform to test structural reinforcement strategies, drivetrain optimization, and system integration under severe dynamic loading conditions.

Design Approach

The chassis was plasma-cut from aluminum plate and configured as a rigid load-bearing frame with reinforced joints to resist high-magnitude impacts. Structural ribs and gusseting were strategically positioned to reduce stress concentrations at motor mounts and corner junctions, which are known weak points in combat environments. The drivetrain featured a custom-designed, 3D-printed belted gearbox, selected over a rigid geartrain for its ability to dampen transient loads while still transmitting ample torque. Simulations were conducted to validate the motor and power delivery system, confirming that battery capacity, pushing power, and free speed met performance requirements for sustained competition. The four-wheel drive layout provided high traction and maneuverability, with the belted reduction optimizing acceleration without sacrificing top-end speed.

Manufacturing

Fabrication combined subtractive and additive processes to balance strength, precision, and adaptability. The chassis plates were plasma-cut to ensure accurate tolerances and minimize material waste, while reinforcement plates were machined to align with SolidWorks GD&T specifications. The drivetrain housings and belted gearbox were 3D printed, allowing rapid iteration during prototyping and effective integration with off-the-shelf pulleys and shafts. Assembly used a hybrid of bolted fasteners for serviceability and welded joints at high-stress interfaces for maximum rigidity. This approach produced a lightweight yet robust structural framework that met the competition‚Äôs strict weight classification.

Testing and Validation

Validation emphasized structural reliability and drivetrain performance. Using SolidWorks FEA, the chassis was analyzed under simulated impact conditions with Von Mises stress plots, safety factor mapping, and displacement analysis guiding reinforcement in critical regions. Buckling studies were conducted on slender frame members to confirm resistance to instability, while simplified fatigue simulations ensured repeated impacts would not initiate crack propagation over multiple matches. The belted gearbox was analyzed for torque transfer and alignment, with emphasis on maintaining belt tension under dynamic loads.
Additional structural studies included buckling analysis of slender frame members to rule out instability under compressive loads, and modal analysis to verify that natural frequencies were well separated from excitation frequencies caused by drivetrain vibration. Simplified fatigue simulations were also carried out to approximate the effect of repeated impacts, ensuring the chassis could endure cyclic stresses without crack initiation.
Physical testing mirrored the simulations. Mobility trials confirmed responsive handling and drivetrain efficiency, while drop-weight and staged impact tests validated that the reinforced chassis absorbed forces without yielding. Extended runtime tests ensured that the motors and battery system delivered consistent torque output while maintaining safe thermal operating limits. The drivetrain in particular demonstrated high responsiveness and ease of control, aligning with predicted performance from simulation.

Results

In competition, the robot consistently outperformed expectations, sustaining repeated direct strikes without structural failure while maintaining full mobility throughout matches. The reinforced chassis successfully absorbed and redistributed impact forces, preventing bending or cracking in critical load-bearing members. The drivetrain proved exceptionally resilient, delivering reliable torque and maneuverability even under repeated collisions. While the absence of an active weapon limited offensive capability, the design‚Äôs defensive robustness allowed it to outlast more aggressive opponents whose systems failed under combat stress. Ultimately, the robot secured 1st place out of 10 competing teams, validating the effectiveness of a durability-first design approach and demonstrating the value of robust chassis reinforcement and drivetrain reliability in combat robotics.
