RC Car

Custom Built Remote-Controlled Racing Vehicle

DFM Validation Control Systems 3D Printing

Project Overview

This project involved designing and building a custom remote-controlled car from scratch, focusing on performance optimization, durability, and advanced control systems for competitive racing applications. The goal was to create a high-performance RC vehicle that could compete in local racing competitions while serving as a learning platform for mechanical design and electronics integration.

Drivetrain

A gear-driven reduction was our baseline, using a two-stage spur arrangement with a 16-tooth pinion on the motor meshing to a 64-tooth gear on the intermediate shaft (‚âà4:1), followed by a 22-tooth gear driving an 88-tooth axle gear (‚âà4:1), for an overall 16:1 ratio. This layout gave strong torque multiplication and near-perfect efficiency (>97%), but it came at the cost of audible gear whine, higher backlash, and sharp load spikes whenever torque reversed. Even with careful attention to center distance, backlash control, and lubrication, the rigid gear mesh transmitted vibrations directly through the drivetrain, which led to occasional tooth edge chipping during abrupt accelerations. From a durability standpoint, the system was mechanically sound, but the harshness and dynamic stresses made it less forgiving in lightweight applications like our RC car.

By contrast, we re-engineered the drivetrain with a synchronous belt system to reduce these drawbacks. The first stage used a 20-tooth HTD pulley on the motor driving a 60-tooth pulley on the jackshaft (‚âà3:1), while the second stage used a 24-tooth pulley to an 84-tooth axle pulley (‚âà3.5:1), giving a total reduction of ~10.5:1‚Äîclose enough to preserve acceleration and stall torque characteristics. Belts were specified at 10 mm width with aramid cords, chosen for their low stretch and high fatigue resistance. With wrap angles held above 160¬∞ using simple idlers, each stage maintained 7‚Äì9 teeth in contact, minimizing tooth shear and ratcheting. Efficiency dropped slightly to ~94%, but the belts absorbed transient loads through elastic deformation, reducing peak stresses by nearly 25% compared to gears. Noise levels dropped substantially, torque delivery became smoother, and vibration-induced wear was essentially eliminated. For rapid prototyping we printed pulleys in PETG at 35% infill, which held up for testing, before moving to machined aluminum for production. Overall, the belt approach traded a small hit in efficiency for far greater reliability, quieter operation, and much lower dynamic loading‚Äîan advantageous compromise for this application.

GD&T

Because FDM printing varies in accuracy, we didn‚Äôt assume ‚ÄúCAD = part.‚Äù For holes and shafts, we used a hole-basis system targeting H7/g7 clearance fits. Bores were modeled slightly undersized (‚âà4.90‚Äì4.94 mm) and reamed to H7; where reaming wasn‚Äôt practical, brass bushings were added. GD&T was applied to ensure the bore was aligned with the pulley OD, circular runout at the teeth stayed within 0.05 mm (to prevent belt walk), and hub faces remained perpendicular. This effectively created a controlled, press-fit-like system that was reliable in plastic. Parts were inspected in batches for bore size, runout, and D-flat width. Steering and Vehicle Performance

We implemented Ackermann steering to align wheel turning radii, reducing scrub and improving maneuverability. CAD predicted a ~320 mm turning radius; experimental measurement was ~347 mm, consistent with tolerance stack-up and compliance. The final car weighed 12.5 N, achieved ~51 minutes per charge, and the belt drivetrain maintained performance without the catastrophic wear observed in the gear mesh. Motor thermal rise during extended runs highlighted a future opportunity for passive cooling and gearing/timing optimization.
